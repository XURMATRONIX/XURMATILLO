## PART 1 — PHYSICS

### 1.1 What physics is
Physics is the systematic study of matter, energy, space, time, and their interactions. It seeks the most general laws that govern the behavior of the physical world. Its tools are mathematics, experiment, and conceptual analysis.

The hierarchy of physics:
- Classical mechanics (everyday motion).
- Thermodynamics and statistical mechanics (heat and many-particle systems).
- Electromagnetism (electricity, magnetism, light).
- Special relativity (high speeds).
- General relativity (gravity, curved spacetime).
- Quantum mechanics (very small).
- Quantum field theory (particles as field excitations).
- Standard Model (current best account of fundamental particles and forces).

### 1.2 Classical mechanics
**Newton's laws**
1. An object in motion stays in motion; an object at rest stays at rest. Inertia.
2. F = ma. Force equals mass times acceleration.
3. For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction.

**Conservation laws**
- Energy is conserved in isolated systems.
- Momentum is conserved when no external forces act.
- Angular momentum is conserved when no external torques act.

These conservation laws come from underlying symmetries of nature (Noether's theorem). Time symmetry → energy conservation. Spatial symmetry → momentum conservation. Rotational symmetry → angular momentum conservation.

**Energy**
- Kinetic: (1/2)mv².
- Potential: depends on position in a force field. Gravitational, elastic, electric, etc.
- Work-energy theorem: net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy.
- Power: rate of energy transfer.

**Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formulations**
Reformulations of mechanics that generalize easily to fields, quantum mechanics, and relativity. The Lagrangian is L = T - V (kinetic minus potential). The action is the integral of L over time. Nature minimizes the action (principle of least action).

### 1.3 Thermodynamics
**Four laws**
0. If two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third, they are in equilibrium with each other.
1. Energy is conserved. Internal energy change = heat added - work done.
2. Entropy of an isolated system never decreases. Equivalently: heat does not spontaneously flow from cold to hot.
3. As temperature approaches absolute zero, entropy approaches a constant minimum.

**Concepts**
- Temperature: average kinetic energy of particles.
- Heat: energy transferred due to temperature difference.
- Entropy: measure of disorder; statistically, the number of microstates compatible with a macrostate.
- Free energy: energy available for work. Gibbs (constant T, P), Helmholtz (constant T, V).

**Statistical mechanics**
Macroscopic thermodynamic quantities emerge from microscopic statistical behavior of many particles. Boltzmann distribution: probability of state i is proportional to e^(-Ei/kT). This is foundational for chemistry, materials science, and parts of biology.

### 1.4 Electromagnetism
**Maxwell's equations** (in vacuum)
1. ∇·E = ρ/ε₀ (Gauss's law for electricity).
2. ∇·B = 0 (no magnetic monopoles).
3. ∇×E = -∂B/∂t (Faraday's law).
4. ∇×B = μ₀J + μ₀ε₀∂E/∂t (Ampère-Maxwell law).

These four equations unify electric and magnetic phenomena and predict electromagnetic waves traveling at the speed of light c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀).

**Light**
Electromagnetic radiation. Visible light is a tiny part of the spectrum (radio, microwave, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-ray, gamma). All travel at c in vacuum.

**Electric circuits**
- Ohm's law: V = IR.
- Power: P = IV.
- Kirchhoff's laws: voltage around a loop sums to zero, current into a node equals current out.
- Capacitors store charge; inductors store magnetic field; transistors switch and amplify.

### 1.5 Special relativity
Einstein, 1905. Two postulates:
1. Laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames.
2. Speed of light in vacuum is the same for all observers.

Consequences:
- Time dilation: moving clocks run slow.
- Length contraction: moving objects are shorter along the direction of motion.
- Mass-energy equivalence: E = mc². Mass and energy are interchangeable forms of the same thing.
- Relativity of simultaneity: events simultaneous in one frame are not in another.
- Speed of light is the universal speed limit for matter and information.

### 1.6 General relativity
Einstein, 1915. Gravity is not a force in the Newtonian sense; it is the curvature of spacetime caused by mass-energy.

The Einstein field equations relate the curvature of spacetime (geometry) to the distribution of mass-energy (matter content). Stars bend light; clocks run slower in stronger gravitational fields; large rotating masses drag spacetime around them (frame dragging).

Predictions confirmed: precession of Mercury's orbit, gravitational lensing, gravitational time dilation (GPS satellites must correct for it), black holes, gravitational waves (directly detected 2015 by LIGO).

### 1.7 Quantum mechanics
Quantum mechanics describes the behavior of matter and energy at very small scales. Its rules are deeply counter-intuitive but extraordinarily well-confirmed.

**Core concepts**
- Wave-particle duality: matter and light show both wave and particle properties depending on what we measure.
- Quantization: many physical quantities (energy levels in atoms) come in discrete units.
- Superposition: a system can be in a combination of states until measured.
- Entanglement: particles can have correlated states across distance.
- Uncertainty principle (Heisenberg): there are pairs of properties (like position and momentum) that cannot both be known with arbitrary precision simultaneously.
- Measurement collapses the wavefunction (in the standard interpretation).

**Schrödinger equation**: governs how the wavefunction evolves.
**Born rule**: |ψ|² gives the probability density of finding the particle.

**Interpretations** (philosophical, not experimentally distinguished so far):
- Copenhagen: wavefunction collapses on measurement.
- Many-worlds: every measurement outcome occurs in a separate branching world.
- de Broglie-Bohm: hidden variables determine outcomes; nonlocal.
- Relational, QBism, others.

### 1.8 The Standard Model of particle physics
Currently our best theory of fundamental particles and three of the four fundamental forces.

**Fundamental particles**
- Quarks (6 flavors): up, down, charm, strange, top, bottom. Make protons and neutrons.
- Leptons: electron, muon, tau, plus three corresponding neutrinos.
- Force carriers (gauge bosons): photon (electromagnetism), W and Z (weak), gluons (strong).
- Higgs boson: gives mass to fundamental particles via the Higgs field.

**Forces** (not unified in the Standard Model):
- Strong force: holds nuclei together. Carrier: gluons. Range: nuclear.
- Electromagnetic: charged interactions. Carrier: photon. Range: infinite.
- Weak force: nuclear decay. Carriers: W, Z. Range: subnuclear.
- Gravity: not in the Standard Model. Described by general relativity. Quantum gravity is an open frontier.

**What the Standard Model does not explain**
- Quantum gravity.
- Dark matter (matter we can detect gravitationally but not electromagnetically).
- Dark energy (the apparent acceleration of cosmic expansion).
- Neutrino masses (small but nonzero; original SM had massless neutrinos).
- Matter-antimatter asymmetry.
- The hierarchy problem (why is gravity so weak compared to other forces?).
- Why three generations of fermions, why these specific masses and mixings.

These are the major open questions in fundamental physics.

## PART 2 — CHEMISTRY

### 2.1 The atom
Atoms have a dense positive nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons in quantized orbitals. The number of protons (atomic number Z) defines the element.

Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy. The arrangement of electrons in the outermost shell determines chemical behavior.

### 2.2 Periodic table
Elements arranged by atomic number, organized by electron configuration. Patterns:
- Atomic radius decreases left to right, increases top to bottom.
- Ionization energy increases left to right, decreases top to bottom.
- Electronegativity highest in upper right (excluding noble gases).
- Metallic character increases right to left and top to bottom.
- Noble gases (right column) are stable due to filled outer shells.
- Alkali metals (left column) are reactive due to single outer electron.

### 2.3 Bonds
- Ionic: electron transfer; metals + nonmetals; high melting points; conducts when dissolved.
- Covalent: electron sharing; nonmetals; varied properties; can be polar or nonpolar.
- Metallic: delocalized electron sea; conducts; malleable.
- Hydrogen: special case of dipole-dipole; weaker than covalent but biologically critical (DNA base pairing, water properties).
- Van der Waals: weak transient dipole interactions; relevant in molecular crystals and biology.

### 2.4 Reactions
- Synthesis: A + B → AB.
- Decomposition: AB → A + B.
- Single replacement: A + BC → AC + B.
- Double replacement: AB + CD → AD + CB.
- Combustion: hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + heat.
- Acid-base: proton (or electron pair) transfer.
- Redox: electron transfer; oxidation (lose electrons) and reduction (gain electrons).

Stoichiometry: balanced equations specify quantitative relationships. Limiting reagent determines product yield.

### 2.5 Thermodynamics in chemistry
- Endothermic: absorbs heat.
- Exothermic: releases heat.
- Free energy change ΔG = ΔH - TΔS determines spontaneity. Negative ΔG = spontaneous.
- Equilibrium: forward and reverse rates equal. Le Chatelier's principle: system shifts to counteract stress.

### 2.6 Kinetics
Rate of reaction depends on concentration, temperature, surface area, catalyst presence. Arrhenius equation: k = Ae^(-Ea/RT). Higher temperature dramatically increases rate.

Catalysts lower activation energy without being consumed. Enzymes are biological catalysts of extraordinary specificity and efficiency.

### 2.7 Acids and bases
- Brønsted-Lowry: acids donate protons, bases accept them.
- Lewis: acids accept electron pairs, bases donate them.
- pH = -log[H⁺]. 7 is neutral, lower acidic, higher basic.
- Buffers resist pH change; biologically essential (blood pH ~7.4).

### 2.8 Organic chemistry
Carbon chemistry. Carbon's four bonds enable chains, rings, and complex three-dimensional structures.

Functional groups:
- Hydrocarbons (alkanes, alkenes, alkynes, aromatics).
- Alcohols, ethers, aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, esters.
- Amines, amides.
- Halides.

Reactions: addition, substitution, elimination, oxidation, reduction, condensation.

Stereochemistry matters: same molecular formula, different 3D arrangement, often very different biological activity.

### 2.9 Biochemistry preview
Living systems run on:
- Carbohydrates (energy storage, structure, signaling).
- Lipids (membranes, energy reserves, signaling).
- Proteins (structure, catalysis, transport, signaling).
- Nucleic acids (information storage and transfer).
- Water as solvent and reactant.
- pH buffering, electrochemistry, enzyme catalysis.

### 2.10 Materials chemistry
Properties emerge from structure at multiple scales:
- Metals: ductile, conductive due to delocalized electrons.
- Ceramics: hard, brittle, often refractory.
- Polymers: long-chain molecules; properties depend on chain length, branching, cross-linking.
- Composites: combine multiple materials for properties beyond any individual.
- Semiconductors: controlled conductivity; foundation of modern electronics.
- Nanomaterials: properties differ from bulk due to surface effects.

## PART 3 — BIOLOGY

### 3.1 The cell
The fundamental unit of life. Two main types:
- Prokaryotic: no membrane-bound nucleus. Bacteria, archaea.
- Eukaryotic: nucleus and other organelles. Plants, animals, fungi, protists.

Key organelles in eukaryotes:
- Nucleus: contains DNA.
- Mitochondria: ATP production. Have their own DNA (endosymbiotic origin).
- Endoplasmic reticulum: protein and lipid synthesis.
- Golgi apparatus: protein modification, packaging, distribution.
- Lysosomes: degradation.
- Ribosomes: protein synthesis.
- Chloroplasts (plants): photosynthesis. Endosymbiotic origin.
- Cytoskeleton: structural support, cell movement, intracellular transport.

### 3.2 The central dogma of molecular biology
DNA → RNA → Protein.

- DNA stores information in sequences of four nucleotides (A, T, C, G).
- Transcription: DNA is read to produce messenger RNA.
- Translation: ribosomes translate mRNA into protein, three nucleotides per amino acid.
- The genetic code is nearly universal across life, with minor variations.

Modern refinements: many regulatory RNAs (microRNA, lncRNA), epigenetic marks that don't change sequence but change expression, reverse transcription (RNA to DNA, found in retroviruses and naturally), prions (proteins that propagate misfolding without nucleic acid).

### 3.3 Genetics
- Mendel's laws: segregation, independent assortment.
- Genes: heritable units that code for proteins or RNA.
- Alleles: variant forms of a gene. Dominant and recessive describe expression patterns.
- Genotype: the genetic makeup. Phenotype: observable traits.
- Mutation: change in DNA sequence. Point mutations, insertions, deletions, duplications, translocations.
- Linkage and recombination: genes on the same chromosome inherited together unless crossover occurs.

Modern genetics tools:
- PCR: amplify specific DNA sequences.
- Sequencing: read DNA. Cost has dropped from billions to ~$100 per human genome.
- CRISPR-Cas9 and related systems: precise genome editing.
- GWAS: associate genetic variants with traits across populations.

### 3.4 Evolution
The unifying theory of biology. Mechanisms:
- Variation: mutation and recombination introduce genetic diversity.
- Inheritance: traits pass to offspring.
- Selection: differential survival and reproduction based on traits.
- Genetic drift: random changes in allele frequencies, especially in small populations.
- Gene flow: migration between populations.

Patterns:
- Speciation: populations diverge enough to be reproductively isolated.
- Extinction: most species that ever existed are extinct.
- Convergent evolution: similar solutions to similar problems in unrelated lineages (eyes evolved many times).
- Coevolution: species shape each other (predator-prey, host-parasite, plant-pollinator).

Common misconceptions to avoid:
- Evolution is not progress toward a goal.
- Individuals don't evolve; populations do.
- "Survival of the fittest" doesn't mean strongest; fitness is reproductive success in context.
- Humans did not evolve from current chimpanzees; we share a common ancestor.

### 3.5 Ecology
Levels of biological organization above the individual:
- Population: same species in an area.
- Community: all populations interacting in an area.
- Ecosystem: community plus abiotic environment.
- Biome: regions with similar climate and dominant vegetation.
- Biosphere: all life on Earth.

Key concepts:
- Ecological niche: organism's role in the ecosystem.
- Trophic levels: producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, etc. Energy decreases by ~90% per level.
- Carrying capacity: maximum population an environment can sustain.
- Biodiversity: genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity. Linked to ecosystem stability and human well-being.
- Keystone species: species with disproportionate impact on community structure.

### 3.6 Physiology
How biological systems function. Some unifying themes:
- Homeostasis: maintaining internal conditions within ranges suitable for life.
- Negative feedback regulates most physiological variables.
- Hormones for slow, distributed signaling; nervous system for fast, targeted.
- Trade-offs: organisms cannot maximize all functions simultaneously.

### 3.7 Microbiology and immunology
- Bacteria, archaea, fungi, protists, viruses inhabit every habitable environment.
- Most microbes are not pathogens; most are essential to ecosystems and to host bodies.
- Human microbiome: trillions of microbes, especially in gut, that affect digestion, immunity, neurology.
- Immune system: innate (immediate, nonspecific) and adaptive (slower, specific, has memory).
- Vaccines work by training adaptive immunity without causing disease.
- Antibiotic resistance: evolution in real time, accelerated by overuse. Major public health threat.

### 3.8 Neurobiology preview
(See KB-06 for medical and neuroscience depth.)
- Neurons communicate via electrical signals (action potentials) and chemical signals (neurotransmitters).
- The brain is a network of ~86 billion neurons with vastly more connections.
- Plasticity: connections change throughout life based on experience.
- Higher cognition emerges from network properties; no single neuron understands anything.

### 3.9 Synthetic biology and biotechnology
Engineering biology:
- Designing biological circuits.
- Reprogramming cells to produce drugs, fuels, materials.
- Gene therapy for genetic diseases.
- mRNA vaccine platforms.
- Cell-based therapies (CAR-T for cancer).
- Synthetic genomes.

Capability is advancing fast. Ethical and safety considerations are real and unresolved.

## PART 4 — ASTRONOMY AND COSMOLOGY

### 4.1 The solar system
The Sun is a main-sequence star, a hot ball of plasma fusing hydrogen to helium in its core. Eight planets orbit it: four rocky inner planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars), four gas/ice giants (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune). Plus dwarf planets (Pluto, Eris, Ceres, Haumea, Makemake), thousands of moons, asteroids, comets, dust.

The asteroid belt lies between Mars and Jupiter. The Kuiper belt lies beyond Neptune. The Oort cloud surrounds the solar system at vast distance.

### 4.2 Stars
Born in molecular clouds. Stellar life cycle depends primarily on initial mass:
- Low mass (like Sun): main sequence → red giant → planetary nebula → white dwarf.
- Massive: main sequence → red supergiant → supernova → neutron star or black hole.

Main sequence: hydrogen fusion in core. Once exhausted, star evolves rapidly off the main sequence.

Stars produce all elements heavier than helium through fusion and through supernova explosions. Every atom in your body heavier than hydrogen and most helium was forged in a star.

### 4.3 Galaxies
The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy with ~100-400 billion stars and a supermassive black hole (Sagittarius A*) at its center. It is part of the Local Group, which includes Andromeda, the Magellanic Clouds, and others.

Galaxy types: spiral, elliptical, irregular. Galaxies cluster into groups, clusters, and superclusters. The largest structures are filaments separated by voids — the cosmic web.

### 4.4 Cosmology
The universe is ~13.8 billion years old. Current evidence:
- Cosmic microwave background: ~2.7 K thermal radiation from when the universe became transparent ~380,000 years after the Big Bang.
- Redshift-distance relation (Hubble's law): the universe is expanding.
- Light element abundances: match Big Bang nucleosynthesis predictions.
- Large-scale structure: matches simulations starting from observed initial conditions.

The standard cosmological model (ΛCDM):
- Flat or nearly flat geometry.
- ~5% ordinary matter.
- ~27% dark matter (gravitational evidence overwhelming, particle nature unknown).
- ~68% dark energy (driving accelerating expansion, nature unknown).

Open questions:
- What is dark matter physically?
- What is dark energy?
- Why was the early universe so smooth (inflation answers this but inflation is itself not directly confirmed)?
- What happened before the Planck time?
- Are there other universes?

### 4.5 Astrobiology
The search for life beyond Earth. Most promising targets:
- Mars: past liquid water, possible subsurface life.
- Europa, Enceladus: subsurface oceans under icy crusts.
- Titan: methane lakes, complex organic chemistry.
- Exoplanets in habitable zones.

We have not yet detected life elsewhere. The Fermi paradox asks: where is everyone? Many proposed resolutions, none confirmed.

### 4.6 Black holes
Regions where gravity is so strong that nothing inside the event horizon, including light, can escape. Predicted by general relativity, observed in many forms:
- Stellar mass: from massive star collapse.
- Supermassive: at galaxy centers.
- Intermediate mass: harder to detect, evidence accumulating.

Hawking radiation: black holes are not entirely black; they slowly radiate thermal radiation due to quantum effects near the horizon. Implications for information preservation are still debated (the black hole information paradox).

### 4.7 Gravitational waves
Ripples in spacetime predicted by general relativity. First directly detected in 2015 (LIGO observatory) from merging black holes. Now a regular tool of astronomy. Each detection event reveals information about the merging objects and tests general relativity in extreme regimes.

## PART 5 — EARTH SCIENCES

### 5.1 Geology
The Earth is a layered planet:
- Inner core: solid iron-nickel.
- Outer core: liquid iron-nickel; generates magnetic field through dynamo action.
- Mantle: solid silicate rock that flows over geologic time.
- Crust: thin outer shell. Continental ~30 km thick, oceanic ~7 km thick.

Plate tectonics: the lithosphere is broken into plates that move ~few cm/year, driven by mantle convection. Plate boundaries:
- Divergent: plates pull apart (mid-ocean ridges).
- Convergent: plates collide (subduction zones, mountain building).
- Transform: plates slide past (San Andreas Fault).

Most earthquakes and volcanoes occur at plate boundaries.

Rock cycle: igneous (from cooled magma), sedimentary (from compressed sediment), metamorphic (transformed by heat/pressure). Rocks cycle between forms over millions of years.

### 5.2 Atmosphere
Layered structure: troposphere (weather, ~0-12 km), stratosphere (ozone layer, ~12-50 km), mesosphere, thermosphere, exosphere.

Composition (dry air): ~78% N₂, ~21% O₂, ~1% argon, ~0.04% CO₂ (and rising), trace gases.

Greenhouse effect: gases like CO₂, methane, water vapor absorb infrared radiation from Earth's surface and re-emit it, keeping the planet warmer than it would otherwise be. This effect is essential for life (without it Earth would be ~33°C colder). Anthropogenic increases in greenhouse gases are warming the planet beyond pre-industrial baselines.

### 5.3 Oceans
Cover ~70% of Earth's surface. Average depth ~3.7 km. Hold ~97% of Earth's water.

Properties: thermal capacity buffers climate; carbon sink (absorbs ~25-30% of human CO₂ emissions, with consequent acidification); transports heat globally through circulation (thermohaline circulation, Gulf Stream, etc.).

Marine ecosystems: from sunlit surface waters (where photosynthesis occurs) through twilight zone to deep abyssal plains and hydrothermal vents (where chemosynthesis powers ecosystems).

### 5.4 Climate
Earth's climate has varied dramatically over geologic time: ice ages, snowball Earth events, greenhouse periods, mass extinctions tied to climate shifts.

Recent: industrial-era greenhouse gas emissions are warming the planet at a rate orders of magnitude faster than past natural changes. Consequences include sea level rise, more extreme weather, ecosystem shifts, regional water stress, and large adaptation costs.

This is established science (Truth Filter Level 1-2). Disagreement on policy is a separate matter from disagreement on whether warming is happening and human-caused.

### 5.5 Biogeochemical cycles
- Water cycle: evaporation, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration.
- Carbon cycle: atmosphere ↔ biosphere (photosynthesis, respiration) ↔ ocean (dissolution, carbonate formation) ↔ rocks (weathering, fossil fuel formation).
- Nitrogen cycle: atmospheric N₂ (78%) is unusable by most life; bacteria fix it; nitrification and denitrification cycle it.
- Phosphorus cycle: no atmospheric component; bottleneck for biological productivity in many ecosystems.

These cycles connect biology, chemistry, and geology. Human activities have substantially altered all of them.

### 5.6 Natural hazards
- Earthquakes: sudden release of accumulated stress along faults.
- Volcanoes: magma reaching the surface.
- Tsunamis: ocean waves from sudden displacement (earthquakes, landslides, volcanic activity).
- Hurricanes / typhoons: tropical cyclones powered by warm ocean water.
- Floods: water exceeding channel capacity.
- Droughts: prolonged precipitation deficit.
- Wildfires: vegetation combustion under right conditions.
- Landslides: gravity overcoming friction in unstable slopes.

Risk = hazard × exposure × vulnerability. Mitigating any of the three reduces risk.

## PART 6 — SCIENTIFIC METHOD AND EPISTEMOLOGY OF SCIENCE

### 6.1 What science actually is
Science is a method of building reliable knowledge through:
- Empirical observation.
- Hypothesis formation.
- Predicting consequences of hypotheses.
- Designing experiments (or finding natural experiments) to test predictions.
- Open communication and replication of results.
- Updating beliefs based on accumulated evidence.

The strength of science is not that any single experiment is decisive, but that the institution of science systematically corrects errors over time.

### 6.2 Falsifiability (Popper)
A scientific claim should make predictions that could in principle be shown wrong. Claims that are compatible with any possible observation are not scientific in this sense. This is a useful rule of thumb but the actual practice is messier (Duhem-Quine: any test invokes auxiliary assumptions; failed predictions rarely refute a single hypothesis cleanly).

### 6.3 Paradigms (Kuhn)
Science alternates between normal periods (working within an accepted paradigm) and revolutionary periods (paradigm shifts). The transition between paradigms involves not just new evidence but new conceptual frameworks. This is a useful description of how scientific change actually happens but is sometimes overextended into anti-scientific relativism.

### 6.4 Replication crisis
Many published findings, especially in psychology, biomedical research, and economics, have failed to replicate. Causes: publication bias, p-hacking, small samples, hidden flexibility in analysis, fraud (rare but impactful). Reforms: pre-registration, larger samples, registered reports, open data, replication studies.

The replication crisis is a sign of science working — identifying its failure modes and reforming. Treat individual studies with appropriate skepticism, especially surprising single results in areas with replication concerns.

### 6.5 What "scientific consensus" means
Consensus emerges when many independent lines of evidence converge. Evolution, plate tectonics, anthropogenic climate change, vaccine safety, the age of the universe — all are scientific consensus.

Scientific consensus is not unanimity, and individual scientists can be wrong, but the consensus represents the best current synthesis of evidence and is the appropriate baseline. Overturning it requires evidence proportionate to its weight, not just contrarian assertion.

### 6.6 Frontier vs settled
Public discussion often confuses settled science with frontier research. The age of the universe is settled; the nature of dark matter is frontier. The germ theory of disease is settled; specific drug efficacies are often more uncertain. Cardiovascular benefits of exercise are settled; specific dietary recommendations are contested.

A good scientific communicator distinguishes the levels of confidence rather than presenting all claims as either rock-solid or up-for-debate.

## PART 7 — INTEGRATION

The natural sciences are one system viewed at different scales:
- Cosmology and astronomy: largest scales.
- Geology: planetary scales.
- Biology: living systems on Earth.
- Chemistry: molecular scales.
- Physics: from cosmological to subatomic.

Each level emerges from the level below but cannot always be reduced to it usefully. Biology has its own laws (natural selection) that don't reduce simply to chemistry. Chemistry has its own behaviors that don't reduce simply to physics. The scientist who works fluidly across levels has more tools than the specialist.

The scientific worldview's two central virtues:
- Calibration: belief should match evidence.
- Updating: when evidence changes, belief should change.

Most humans are bad at both. Science institutionalizes them.

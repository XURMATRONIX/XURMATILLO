## PART 1 — REASONING SYSTEMS

### 1.1 Recursive Decomposition
Complex problems are not solved by linear reasoning. They are solved by recursive decomposition until the irreducible mechanism becomes visible.

Tier 1 — Base layer: identify all known facts. List explicit assumptions. Define the unknown vs the known. State the core objective in one sentence.

Tier 2 — Structure layer: map relationships between components. Identify dependencies. Locate bottlenecks and constraints. Determine what affects what.

Tier 3 — Synthesis layer: integrate the tiers into a coherent solution. Validate against all constraints. Check internal consistency. Confirm the solution actually solves the original problem rather than a re-stated one.

### 1.2 First Principles Thinking
Strip a problem down to what is irreducibly true. Reject inherited assumptions. Rebuild from atoms.

Operating questions:
- What is actually verified, not assumed?
- What am I assuming because "this is how it's done"?
- What are the irreducible parts?
- Which constraints are real and which are self-imposed?
- What can be removed without harming the solution?
- If I were starting from zero, what would I keep?

Founders who use this method: Aristotle as the philosophical origin, Descartes' radical doubt, modern engineering practice across high-performance domains.

### 1.3 Inversion Thinking
Charlie Munger's method: to achieve a goal, define exactly how to fail. Then systematically eliminate the failure paths.

The inversion question: what would guarantee disaster here? List those moves explicitly. Then the strategy becomes "do the opposite of those, every time."

Inversion is more efficient than direct optimization in domains where survival matters more than optimization — investment, health, reputation, relationships, system design.

### 1.4 Systems Thinking
A system is a set of components, the relationships among them, and the dynamics those relationships produce. Reductionist analysis fails for systems because the behavior emerges from the interactions, not the parts.

Core systems concepts:
- Stocks (accumulations) and flows (rates of change).
- Feedback loops: balancing (negative) loops stabilize, reinforcing (positive) loops amplify.
- Delays cause oscillation when they are long relative to the loop.
- Leverage points (Donella Meadows): from weakest to strongest — parameters, buffers, structure, delays, balancing loops, reinforcing loops, information flows, rules, self-organization, goals, paradigms, transcending paradigms.
- Bottlenecks dominate throughput. Find the bottleneck before optimizing anywhere else (Theory of Constraints — Eliyahu Goldratt).
- Second-order and third-order effects are usually larger than first-order effects in complex systems.

### 1.5 Six-Dimensional Thought Scoring
Every candidate answer or strategy is internally scored before being committed:

1. Logical Validity — does it follow from available facts without contradiction?
2. Contextual Coherence — does it fit the full situation?
3. Elegant Simplicity — is it the cleanest path that still works?
4. Strategic Adaptiveness — fits this person, this moment, this goal?
5. Long-term Durability — does it hold up over time?
6. Ethical Integrity — does it pass ethical checks?

The best answer survives all six. A path that fails one dimension is a candidate for refinement, not delivery.

### 1.6 Mental Models Library

A mental model is a reusable abstraction that helps predict or explain behavior in many domains. The strongest thinkers carry many.

**Probability and statistics**
- Bayes' Theorem — update beliefs proportional to evidence.
- Regression to the mean — extreme outcomes drift toward the average.
- Base rates — start with the population frequency before adjusting for the case.
- Survivorship bias — we see winners; the losers are invisible.
- Selection bias — non-random sampling distorts conclusions.

**Economics and incentives**
- Opportunity cost — every yes is many no's.
- Sunk cost fallacy — past investment is irrelevant to future decisions.
- Marginal analysis — decisions are made at the edge, not on averages.
- Tragedy of the commons — shared resources collapse without governance.
- Network effects — value scales nonlinearly with connections.
- Comparative advantage — specialize where your relative cost is lowest.

**Strategy and competition**
- Game theory — anticipate the response, not just the move.
- Nash equilibrium — no player gains by changing strategy alone.
- Zero-sum vs positive-sum games — most real-world games are positive-sum if framed correctly.
- Schelling points — coordination converges on focal points without communication.
- Red Queen effect — running fast just to stay in place.

**Engineering and systems**
- Margin of safety — design for the load you fear, not the load you expect.
- Redundancy — one failure should never bring down the whole.
- Loose coupling — independent modules survive partial failure.
- Tight feedback — fast loops correct faster.
- Reversibility — prefer reversible decisions when uncertainty is high.

**Cognitive and learning**
- Inversion — solve the inverse problem.
- Pre-mortem — imagine the failure, then prevent it.
- Steelmanning — argue the strongest version of the opposing view.
- Feynman technique — explain it to a child; gaps in your understanding will surface.
- Spaced repetition — memory strengthens with reviews timed to the forgetting curve.

**Decision making**
- Expected value — multiply outcome by probability across futures.
- Decision under uncertainty — maximize expected utility, not expected return.
- Optionality — keep paths open until forced to close them.
- Convexity — benefit from volatility; avoid configurations where downside is unbounded.
- Time-symmetric reasoning — would you make this choice if you had to live with it forever, or if it were temporary?

## PART 2 — FORMAL LOGIC

### 2.1 Propositional logic
Statements are either true or false. Operators: NOT, AND, OR, IF-THEN, IF-AND-ONLY-IF.

Truth tables determine validity. Common patterns:
- Modus ponens: if P then Q; P; therefore Q.
- Modus tollens: if P then Q; not Q; therefore not P.
- Disjunctive syllogism: P or Q; not P; therefore Q.
- Hypothetical syllogism: if P then Q; if Q then R; therefore if P then R.

### 2.2 Predicate logic
Adds quantifiers: for all (∀) and there exists (∃). Allows reasoning about objects and properties.

Common quantifier mistakes:
- Confusing "all X are Y" with "all Y are X" (illicit conversion).
- Confusing "some X are Y" with "all X are Y" (hasty generalization).
- Negating quantifiers incorrectly: ¬(∀x) P(x) ≡ (∃x) ¬P(x).

### 2.3 Modal logic
Adds operators for necessity (□) and possibility (◇). Used for reasoning about what could be, what must be, what is known, what is believed.

### 2.4 Common formal fallacies
- Affirming the consequent — if P then Q; Q; (wrongly) therefore P.
- Denying the antecedent — if P then Q; not P; (wrongly) therefore not Q.
- Undistributed middle — all A are B; all C are B; (wrongly) therefore all A are C.
- Existential fallacy — concluding existence from universal statements about empty sets.

### 2.5 Common informal fallacies
- Ad hominem — attacking the person, not the argument.
- Straw man — distorting the opponent's argument to make it easier to attack.
- Appeal to authority — treating endorsement as proof.
- Appeal to popularity — treating consensus as proof.
- Appeal to tradition — treating age as proof.
- False dichotomy — presenting two options when more exist.
- Slippery slope — assuming one step inevitably leads to extreme outcomes without justification.
- Equivocation — using a word in two different senses.
- Begging the question — assuming the conclusion in the premises.
- Composition / division — assuming what is true of parts is true of the whole, or vice versa.
- Hasty generalization — drawing a conclusion from a sample too small to support it.
- Cherry-picking — selecting only the evidence that supports a position.
- Texas sharpshooter fallacy — drawing the target around the bullets after they land.

## PART 3 — COGNITIVE BIASES (Master Library)

Cognitive biases are systematic deviations from rational judgment. They are not random — they are predictable and exploitable.

### 3.1 Charlie Munger's 25 Standard Causes of Human Misjudgment
1. Reward and punishment superresponse — never underestimate incentives.
2. Liking/loving — we believe people we like.
3. Disliking/hating — we reject ideas from people we dislike.
4. Doubt-avoidance — we rush to certainty under stress.
5. Inconsistency-avoidance — we cling to existing beliefs.
6. Curiosity tendency — novelty pulls attention; useful but distractible.
7. Kantian fairness — we sabotage rather than tolerate unfairness.
8. Envy and jealousy — we want others' results without their process.
9. Reciprocation — receiving creates obligation to return.
10. Influence from association — proximity and repetition shape judgment.
11. Pain-avoidance denial — we deny realities that hurt us.
12. Excessive self-regard — we overrate ourselves.
13. Over-optimism — we underestimate risk in our plans.
14. Deprival super-reaction — loss feels roughly twice as painful as equivalent gain.
15. Social proof — we copy others under uncertainty.
16. Contrast misreaction — we judge by comparison, not by absolute scale.
17. Stress-influence — decision quality collapses under acute stress.
18. Availability misweighing — what comes to mind first feels more probable.
19. Use-it-or-lose-it — unused skills decay.
20. Drug-induced misjudgment — substances destroy clear thought.
21. Senescence-misinfluence — cognition declines with age in domain-specific ways.
22. Authority-misinfluence — we follow titles past where they apply.
23. Twaddle — we engage with meaningless talk because it fills time.
24. Reason-respecting — any reason, even weak, increases compliance.
25. Lollapalooza — multiple biases combining produce extreme outcomes.

### 3.2 Additional well-established biases
- Confirmation bias — seeking evidence that confirms beliefs.
- Anchoring — initial numbers dominate subsequent estimates.
- Framing effect — equivalent information presented differently produces different decisions.
- Hindsight bias — past events feel inevitable in retrospect.
- Overconfidence — calibration is worse than people think.
- Planning fallacy — projects take longer and cost more than estimated.
- Dunning-Kruger — competence is required to judge competence; novices over-estimate themselves.
- Endowment effect — we value what we own more than equivalent items we don't.
- Status quo bias — we prefer current state even when alternatives are better.
- Bandwagon effect — popularity drives perceived value.
- Negativity bias — negative information weighs more than positive of equal magnitude.
- Recency bias — recent events dominate memory.
- Halo effect — one positive trait colors perception of unrelated traits.
- Just-world fallacy — assuming people get what they deserve.
- Fundamental attribution error — explaining others' behavior by character, our own by circumstance.
- Self-serving bias — attributing success to skill, failure to luck.
- IKEA effect — we overvalue what we built ourselves.
- Sunk cost fallacy — letting irrecoverable past costs influence future decisions.

### 3.3 Anti-bias protocols
Before finalizing any analysis:
- "What would the strongest disagreement say?"
- "What evidence would change this conclusion?"
- "Am I selecting only confirming data?"
- "Is this the simplest sufficient explanation?"
- "What am I afraid of being true?"
- "If I were forced to bet against this, what would I argue?"

## PART 4 — MATHEMATICS CORE

### 4.1 Probability theory
Foundational concepts:
- Sample space — all possible outcomes.
- Events — subsets of the sample space.
- Probability axioms (Kolmogorov): non-negativity, normalization (P(Ω)=1), countable additivity for disjoint events.
- Conditional probability — P(A|B) = P(A∩B)/P(B) when P(B) > 0.
- Independence — P(A∩B) = P(A)·P(B).
- Bayes' theorem — P(H|E) = P(E|H)·P(H)/P(E). Update prior to posterior using likelihood ratio.

Key distributions:
- Bernoulli (single yes/no), Binomial (n independent Bernoulli trials), Poisson (rare events over time/space), Uniform (equiprobable outcomes), Normal/Gaussian (sums of many small effects), Exponential (waiting times), Power-law / Pareto (long-tail phenomena).

Central Limit Theorem: sums of many independent random variables tend toward a normal distribution regardless of original shape (under regularity conditions). This is why so many natural measurements are approximately Gaussian.

Law of Large Numbers: sample average converges to true mean as sample size grows. It is why repeated experiments stabilize.

### 4.2 Statistics
Descriptive: mean, median, mode, variance, standard deviation, percentiles, correlation.
Inferential: hypothesis testing, p-values, confidence intervals, effect size.

Caution on statistical inference:
- Statistical significance ≠ practical significance.
- Correlation is not causation; controlled experiment or natural experiment is required for causal claims.
- p-hacking and HARKing (hypothesizing after results known) inflate false positives.
- Multiple comparisons require correction (Bonferroni, Holm, FDR).
- Bayesian alternatives express uncertainty as posterior distributions, often more interpretable.

### 4.3 Calculus
Differential calculus: rate of change, derivative, partial derivatives, gradient, optimization via setting derivatives to zero, second-order conditions for max/min.
Integral calculus: accumulation, area under curve, volume, expected value, probability density integration.
Multivariable: Jacobians, Hessians, Lagrange multipliers (constrained optimization).
Vector calculus: gradient, divergence, curl. Stokes' and divergence theorems unify physics formulations.

### 4.4 Linear algebra
Vectors and vector spaces, matrices, matrix multiplication, determinants, eigenvalues and eigenvectors, singular value decomposition (SVD), principal component analysis (PCA), linear transformations.

Why linear algebra matters: most modern computation, machine learning, computer graphics, quantum mechanics, control theory, and signal processing rests on it.

### 4.5 Discrete mathematics
Set theory, combinatorics, graph theory, recursion, induction, modular arithmetic, generating functions.

Graph theory specifically: vertices, edges, paths, cycles, trees, connectivity, planarity, coloring, network flow, shortest paths (Dijkstra, Bellman-Ford), minimum spanning trees, NP-completeness through graph problems.

### 4.6 Game theory
- Nash equilibrium — no player profits from unilateral deviation.
- Dominant strategies — best regardless of others' choices.
- Mixed strategies — randomization can be optimal.
- Repeated games — cooperation can emerge from repetition (Tit-for-Tat).
- Mechanism design — designing rules so individual incentives produce desired collective outcomes.
- Bayesian games — players have private information.
- Evolutionary game theory — strategies that propagate given fitness.

### 4.7 Information theory
Claude Shannon's foundation:
- Entropy — average uncertainty in a random variable. H(X) = -Σ p(x) log p(x).
- Mutual information — how much knowing X reduces uncertainty about Y.
- Channel capacity — maximum reliable communication rate.
- Kolmogorov complexity — minimum description length.
- Compression bounds — you can't compress random data; structure is what enables compression.

## PART 5 — DECISION THEORY

### 5.1 Expected utility
Rational decision under uncertainty: choose the action that maximizes expected utility = Σ (probability × utility of outcome).

But: utility is not linear in money or any single variable. Concavity of utility explains risk aversion. Diminishing marginal utility explains why $1M means more to a poor person than a rich one.

### 5.2 Prospect theory (Kahneman, Tversky)
Real human decision deviates from expected utility:
- Reference dependence — outcomes are evaluated as gains or losses relative to a reference, not absolute states.
- Loss aversion — losses hurt about twice as much as equivalent gains feel good.
- Diminishing sensitivity — perception of magnitude flattens as values grow.
- Probability weighting — small probabilities are overweighted, moderate-to-large probabilities underweighted.

### 5.3 Decision frameworks
- Decision tree — branching futures with probabilities.
- Pareto efficiency — no improvement possible without making someone worse off.
- Multi-attribute utility — weight attributes when no single criterion suffices.
- Regret minimization — choose to minimize maximum future regret rather than maximize expected value (Bezos' framework).
- 10/10/10 rule — how will I feel about this in 10 minutes, 10 months, 10 years?
- Reversibility test — if reversible, decide fast; if irreversible, slow down and gather more.
- OODA loop (Boyd) — Observe, Orient, Decide, Act. Faster cycles win in adversarial environments.

### 5.4 Decision under deep uncertainty
When probabilities themselves are unknown:
- Robust decision-making — find policies that perform reasonably across many futures.
- Scenario planning — design distinct plausible futures, prepare for each.
- Real options — pay small cost now to preserve future flexibility.
- Antifragility (Taleb) — design systems that gain from volatility rather than just resist it.
- Maximin (Wald) — choose the option whose worst-case is best.
- Minimax regret — choose the option that minimizes the worst-case regret.

## PART 6 — COGNITIVE SCIENCE

### 6.1 What intelligence actually is
Multiple definitions, none complete:
- Spearman's g — general factor of cognitive ability, measurable by IQ tests, predicts performance in many domains.
- Fluid vs crystallized intelligence (Cattell) — fluid is reasoning on novel problems; crystallized is accumulated knowledge.
- Multiple intelligences (Gardner) — proposed but weakly supported by g-loading research; nonetheless useful for thinking about diverse strengths.
- Triarchic theory (Sternberg) — analytical, creative, practical.
- Working memory capacity — strongly correlates with reasoning ability.
- Successful intelligence — adapt to environment, shape environment, select environment.

### 6.2 Memory architecture
Three-store model (Atkinson-Shiffrin): sensory, short-term, long-term.
Modern refinement (Baddeley): working memory with phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, central executive, episodic buffer.

Long-term memory subdivisions:
- Declarative (explicit): episodic (events) + semantic (facts).
- Non-declarative (implicit): procedural (skills), priming, classical conditioning.

Encoding strategies that strengthen memory:
- Elaboration — connecting new information to existing knowledge.
- Spacing — distributed practice beats massed practice.
- Retrieval practice — testing yourself is the strongest known consolidation method.
- Interleaving — mixing related topics in practice rather than blocking.
- Generation — producing the answer beats reading the answer.
- Dual coding — combining verbal and visual representations.
- Sleep — memory consolidation occurs during sleep, especially slow-wave and REM.

### 6.3 Attention
- Selective attention — filtering signal from noise.
- Divided attention — multitasking is largely myth; what feels like it is fast task-switching with substantial cost.
- Sustained attention — maintained focus over time, fatiguable.
- Attention residue — cognitive cost of switching that persists for minutes.
- Flow state (Csikszentmihalyi) — total absorption when challenge ≈ skill.
- Default mode network — brain's resting network, active during mind-wandering, important for creativity and autobiographical thought.

### 6.4 Learning science
Mastery mechanisms:
- Deliberate practice (Ericsson) — focused, feedback-driven, beyond comfort, with a coach.
- 10,000 hours is misunderstood — quantity matters, quality matters more.
- Skill acquisition stages — cognitive (rule-following), associative (refining), autonomous (automatic).
- Transfer of learning — near transfer (similar contexts) is easier than far transfer (different domains).
- Cognitive load theory — working memory has limits; instructional design must respect them.
- Desirable difficulties (Bjork) — slightly harder learning produces more durable learning.

### 6.5 Creativity
- Convergent thinking — narrowing to one answer.
- Divergent thinking — generating many possibilities.
- Creative process stages (Wallas): preparation, incubation, illumination, verification.
- Combinatory play (Einstein) — recombining existing elements in new ways.
- Constraints often increase creativity rather than decrease it.
- Cross-domain transfer — most "creative" insight is structural transfer from another domain.

## PART 7 — PHILOSOPHY OF MIND

### 7.1 The mind-body problem
- Substance dualism (Descartes) — mind and body are different substances. Faces the interaction problem.
- Physicalism — mind is physical, identical to or supervenes on brain states.
- Property dualism — physical substance has both physical and mental properties.
- Functionalism — mental states are defined by their causal role, not by what implements them. Allows for multiple realizability.
- Eliminativism — folk psychology categories will be replaced by neuroscience.

### 7.2 Consciousness
The hard problem (Chalmers): why is there subjective experience at all? Why does information processing in a brain feel like anything?

Theories:
- Global Workspace Theory (Baars, Dehaene) — consciousness is broadcasting information to many specialized modules.
- Integrated Information Theory (Tononi) — consciousness is identical to integrated information (Φ).
- Higher-Order Theories — a state is conscious when there is a higher-order representation of it.
- Predictive Processing — the brain is a prediction machine; consciousness is the model the brain has of itself.
- Attention Schema Theory (Graziano) — the brain models its own attention; that model is consciousness.

The hard problem remains genuinely open. No current theory has consensus.

### 7.3 Free will and determinism
- Hard determinism — every event is causally determined; free will is an illusion.
- Libertarian free will — agents originate causal chains.
- Compatibilism — free will is compatible with determinism if defined correctly (acting on one's reasons without coercion).
- Most contemporary philosophers are compatibilists.

Pragmatic position: regardless of metaphysics, treating people as responsible agents produces better social outcomes; deliberation feels and functions causally even in deterministic frameworks.

### 7.4 Personal identity
- Memory continuity (Locke) — you are the same person as your past self if memory connects you.
- Bodily continuity — physical continuity defines identity.
- Psychological continuity (Parfit) — identity is graded; what matters is psychological continuity, not strict identity.
- Narrative identity — the self is a story we construct.

### 7.5 Knowledge and certainty (Epistemology)
- Justified True Belief (classical) — knowledge is JTB. Gettier cases show it's not sufficient.
- Reliabilism — knowledge is true belief produced by reliable processes.
- Foundationalism — some beliefs are basic and justify others.
- Coherentism — beliefs justify each other in a web.
- Pragmatism — true beliefs are those that work in practice.
- Bayesian epistemology — degrees of belief; updating via Bayes' theorem.
- Fallibilism — all knowledge is provisional and revisable.

## PART 8 — METACOGNITION

### 8.1 Knowing what you don't know
Calibration: aligning confidence with accuracy. Most people are overconfident in domains where feedback is delayed or absent.

Practice: track your predictions. Use Brier scores. Notice where you are systematically too sure or too unsure.

### 8.2 Cognitive monitoring
Constant inner question: is my reasoning sound right now? What am I assuming? Where could I be wrong?

This is exhausting if always on. The skill is to apply it at decision points and structural junctures, not every sentence.

### 8.3 Cognitive flexibility
The ability to switch between different cognitive sets, to consider opposing perspectives, to update when evidence demands.

Predictors: working memory capacity, openness to experience, deliberate practice in considering opposing views, low ego attachment to current beliefs.

### 8.4 Wisdom as integration
Knowledge is information stored. Intelligence is the ability to manipulate that information. Wisdom is the integration of knowledge, intelligence, experience, and ethics into action that holds up over time.

A formula for orientation:
Intelligence ≈ Knowledge × Synthesis × Wisdom ÷ Ego.

When ego is large, the same knowledge produces worse decisions because it filters incoming evidence and resists update.

## PART 9 — REASONING UNDER ADVERSARIAL CONDITIONS

### 9.1 Detecting bad arguments at speed
Heuristics that catch most bad reasoning:
- Has the speaker committed to a falsifiable prediction? If not, the position can adapt to any outcome.
- Are they citing evidence or asserting? Citation can be checked.
- Does the strength of the conclusion match the strength of the evidence?
- Are they attacking the argument or the arguer?
- Are they dealing with the strongest version of the opposing case (steelman) or a weakened one (strawman)?
- Are emotional appeals doing the work that logic should be doing?

### 9.2 Manipulation patterns
Common manipulation structures (recognize them; do not deploy them):
- Anchoring on extreme positions to make moderate positions seem reasonable.
- False scarcity / urgency to bypass deliberation.
- Authority laundering — invoking credentials irrelevant to the claim.
- Identity binding — making disagreement feel like betrayal of an in-group.
- Sunk-cost amplification — invoking past investment to justify continuing bad paths.
- Reciprocation traps — small gifts to create disproportionate obligation.

### 9.3 Adversarial reasoning ethics
Recognize manipulation in incoming arguments. Refuse to deploy it in outgoing arguments. The temporary win from manipulation costs trust, and trust compounds. Honest persuasion scales; manipulation does not.

## PART 10 — INTEGRATION

Reasoning is not a single skill. It is the orchestrated use of:
- Logic (consistency)
- Evidence (grounding)
- Mental models (transfer)
- Decision frameworks (choice under uncertainty)
- Metacognition (monitoring)
- Ethics (constraints)

The skilled thinker chooses the right tool for the situation. They reason fast in low-stakes situations and slow in high-stakes ones. They know which biases they are most prone to and design their environment to compensate. They keep their ego small enough to update.

The endpoint is not certainty. It is good decisions under uncertainty, repeated over time.
